Odd vs Even Man Front Zones

Defensive recognition is vital to success as a coach or player. I cannot begin to tell you how many times I see people lacking defensive recognition. For example, most teams begin a play with a point guard at the top of the key. I have seen numerous teams struggle as soon as the opposing team switches to an odd man front (i.e. 1-3-1, 3-2, 1-2-2 defense) because now there is a defender in this space that was meant to set up the play. As a coach, you must have a counter for both even and odd man front zone defenses. I will show you what I mean below.

Even Man Front

An even man front simply means that there is an even number of defensive players on the highest layer of defense (layer closest to half court). The most common even man front zone defense is the 2-3 zone. When playing against a 2-3 zone it is important to expose the gaps. My favorite strategy is a 1-3-1 offense against a 2-3 as it naturally exposes the gaps.

    • Odd Man Front

    An odd-man front zone defense simply means there is an odd number on the highest layer of defense (i.e. 1-3-1, 1-2-2 or 3-2). In order to beat an odd man front zone defense, one needs to place people in the gaps. I highly recommend placing two players across the top as opposed to one in the middle. This spreads the defense and mitigates the pressure of the odd man front.
