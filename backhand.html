Tennis Backhand

A dependable backhand is built on balance, timing, and efficient movement.

Key Backhand Fundamentals

Whether one-handed or two-handed, strong backhands rely on preparation, spacing, and clean contact.

Grip & Preparation

  • There are two main variations of the backhand. The two-handed and the one-handed backhand. Depending on which one you choose will determine which grip you use. The videos below discuss the grips of each choice. One important tip when you use a two-handed backhand is that you have to allow your non-dominant hand to be the dominant, power producing, and in-control hand. As simple as that sounds, not everyone does it!

    • Recognition & Footwork

  • One helpful cue that has always helped me is to mirror forehand movements for the backhand and this begins with the feet. Turning side to target (on the opposite side) is key here. Many players try to avoid hitting a backhand early on and this causes them to resist turning to their backhand side. Players need to recognize when they will need to hit a backhand and then make turning to that side second nature. As they get better at turning to this side, they will eventually begin to coil as they turn side to target and step back foot first followed by front foot.

    • Set Position

  • Similar to the forehand, the set position is the position in which we are preparing to hit the ball. For beginners, this is the side-to-target position (at least if we are thinking about closed or neutral stance). We will have the racket cocked back in this position.

    • Swinging Low to High

  • After we are prepared in the set position we will prepare to swing. Continue with the theme of mirroring the forehand. We will swing low to high here or from our hip to the opposite shoulder.

    • Contact Point

  • When we strike the ball we want to make contact slightly out in front of us and towards our backhand side.

    • Follow-Through & Recovery

  • Follow through by allowing the swing to continue upward and over the shoulder.
